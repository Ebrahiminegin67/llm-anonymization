Multi-Round Adversarial Loop: Privacy Metrics by Round

How it works: each round runs a fresh parallel attack on the current anonymized text, then anonymizes again using those new findings. If writing style encodes demographic signals that survive one round of anonymization, repeated rounds should progressively strip them — or confirm that the floor is truly irreducible.

This is an SVG line chart showing Top-3 adversarial accuracy after anonymization for each of the 5 rounds. The red dashed line labelled "55% floor" is the reference line, this is what all single-round architectures achieved. It is drawn there so you can immediately see whether multi-round broke through it.

The blue line sits below the red dashed floor line for 4 out of 5 rounds, meaning the loop did break through the 55% floor. Round 4 is the exception where accuracy briefly jumped back to 55%.

Across all five rounds, post-anonymization Top-3 adversarial accuracy holds between 50% and 55%, breaking below the 55% single-round floor in four out of five rounds. The most consistent trend is in the evidence rate, which declines progressively from 60% post-anonymization in Round 1 down to 30% in Rounds 4 and 5 — a 30 percentage point reduction over the loop. This means that while the attacker's overall guessing rate remains stubbornly similar, it is increasingly guessing without direct textual evidence rather than reading answers from the text. Average certainty also declined from 2.10 in Round 1 to 1.70 by Round 5, confirming the attacker is becoming less confident with each iteration. Combined utility remained perfectly stable at 0.937 across every round without a single drop, demonstrating that five iterations of anonymization editing did not degrade readability or semantic meaning. The Round 4 anomaly — where accuracy briefly rose to 55% — reflects the small sample size (n=20): a single profile's predictions shifting can move the overall accuracy by 5 percentage points.
