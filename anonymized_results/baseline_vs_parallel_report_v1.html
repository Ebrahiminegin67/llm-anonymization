Certainty Analysis

Certainty is the model's self-reported confidence (1–5 scale) that its PII guess is correct. Pre-Anon = certainty before anonymization. Post-Anon = certainty after anonymization. Certainty Drop = how much the anonymizer reduced the attacker's confidence. A larger drop means stronger protection.

Key Weaknesses Identified in v1

  • Attack B failed on 7/20 profiles — Claude added markdown formatting (e.g. **Type: gender**) that the parser could not read, resulting in MISSING responses
  • Merge always used Attack A's order — even when Attack B had higher certainty, A's guesses were placed first in the merged output
  • Anonymizer only saw one inference — it received no secondary reasoning, no agreement signal, and no combined certainty score, so it did not know it needed to defend against two different attack strategies
  • Certainty drop near zero (0.15) — direct consequence of the anonymizer being under-informed

Verdict

The parallel pipeline (v1) defeats 40% of Attack A attempts and 35% of both attacks simultaneously vs the baseline's 15% — a meaningful improvement, but held back by Attack B's unreliability and the anonymizer receiving insufficient information.

These weaknesses directly motivated the v2 improvements: a stricter Attack B prompt, certainty-ordered merging, and a richer anonymizer context that includes both inferences and an agreement signal.

Key Findings

1. The parallel pipeline defeats attacks more often: 40% vs 15%

Even in v1, the merged inference gives the anonymizer better information than a single attack. Attack A defeat rate (40%) is already 2.7x better than the baseline (15%). However, the improvement is limited by how little information the anonymizer actually receives.

2. Attack B is unreliable — 7/20 profiles returned MISSING

Claude added markdown formatting to its responses which the parser could not read. This means 7 profiles had no sociolinguistic inference at all. Attack B's 50% defeat rate is therefore inflated — it counts empty-vs-empty comparisons as defeats.

3. The anonymizer was under-informed

The certainty drop of only 0.15 (vs baseline's 0.70) reveals the core problem: the anonymizer received only Attack A's inference with no secondary perspective and no agreement signal. It had no reason to rewrite the text more aggressively than in the baseline.

4. One profile (47female) was better protected by the baseline

The baseline defeated the attack on 47female (location) but neither parallel attack did. This happened because the parallel pipeline's anonymizer, being under-informed, failed to remove the location signal that the single-model baseline happened to catch.

Bottom Line

v1 shows the parallel architecture has real potential — 40% defeat rate vs 15% baseline — but is held back by three implementation weaknesses: Attack B's MISSING responses, naive merge ordering, and an under-informed anonymizer. These weaknesses were directly fixed in v2, which improved certainty drop by 3–5x and brought both attacks to equal defeat rates of 45%.

Methodology Notes

  • Baseline: Standard sequential pipeline — single GPT-4o inference → anonymize → utility → re-inference (2 iterations)
  • Parallel v1: GPT-4o (analytical) + Claude Sonnet (sociolinguistic, original prompt) parallel inference → A-first merge → anonymize with single inference → utility → parallel re-inference
  • Defeated: No overlap between pre- and post-anonymization guesses (attacker completely misdirected)
  • Agreement: Whether the two parallel attacks agree on guesses before anonymization
  • Same data: Both pipelines ran on the same 20 profiles from inference_0.jsonl with identical filters
